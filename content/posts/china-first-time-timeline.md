You're going to China. You've booked the flight. And now you're lying in bed at midnight, 14 tabs open, every one telling you something different.

"Cash is useless." "No, you need cash." "Get an eSIM." "Get a physical SIM." "Alipay is all you need." "Alipay won't work for you."

I read hundreds of Reddit threads on r/ChinaTravel and r/travel so you don't have to. Here's the truth: **none of this is hard. It's just annoying if you do it in the wrong order.**

This is the timeline. Do these things when they say, and you'll land with a working phone, a working wallet, and a working map.

---

## 30 Days Before: The Things That Need Lead Time

### Book your attractions

The Forbidden City, the National Museum, the Harbin Ice Festival — these book out 7+ days in advance. Some only release tickets in specific windows. Check now. Book now.

### Check your passport validity

Your passport must be valid for at least 6 months beyond your arrival date. China is strict about this. If it expires in 5 months, renew it today.

### Apply for your visa (if needed)

Processing times vary by country and season. For US citizens, the 10-year tourist visa (L) takes 4-7 business days in normal season, but the application itself takes time — photos, forms, invitation letters if applicable.

## 14 Days Before: The Tech Stack You Can't Set Up After You Land

This section matters more than anything else on this list. Here's why: most of the websites, app stores, and services you need to set up are **blocked in China**. If you don't do this at home on your own WiFi, you physically cannot do it.

### Install your eSIM

**Do not wait until you land.** The eSIM provider's website — blocked. The QR code page — blocked. The activation server — you get the idea.

Install and activate the eSIM on your home WiFi before you fly. Turn off data roaming on your home SIM. Set the eSIM as your data line. If you're not sure which eSIM to get:

→ **[Compare China eSIMs for your trip](/posts/best-esim-for-china-travel/)**

### Install a VPN

You might not need it — some travelers report that with an eSIM using a foreign carrier, Google services work without VPN. But hotel WiFi? Cafes? The airport? Those block everything.

Install your VPN app, log in, test it works. If you wait until you're in China to download the VPN app, the download page will be blocked. Full VPN guide:

→ **[Do you need a VPN for China?](/posts/vpn-for-china-travel-2026/)**

### Set up Alipay and complete passport verification

This takes 10 minutes and saves hours of standing at a restaurant counter, phone dead, unable to pay. Link your Visa or Mastercard. Complete the passport verification — it requires taking a photo of your passport and your face. Do this at home. Full step-by-step:

→ **[Alipay setup guide for foreigners](/posts/alipay-foreigner-setup-guide/)**

### Download WeChat

Even if you use Alipay for payments, WeChat is China's universal communication app. Hotels use it. Tour guides use it. Anyone you meet will ask for your WeChat. Download it, register with your foreign number.

### Download Google Translate offline Chinese pack

You won't always have signal. Download the Chinese language pack for offline translation. The screenshot/camera translation in Google Translate works well enough for menus, signs, and short conversations.

### Download a map app

Google Maps is unreliable in China — streets are offset by hundreds of meters. On iPhone, Apple Maps works well. On Android, download Amap (高德地图). Full guide:

## 7 Days Before: The Paperwork and Money

### Print your documents

- Passport photo page (2 copies)
- Visa page
- Hotel booking confirmation (first night)
- Return flight ticket
- Travel insurance policy

Border officers rarely ask for all of them. If they do, having paper gets you through in 30 seconds.

### Save key addresses in Chinese characters

Hotel name and address. Train station. Major attractions. Save them in a note on your phone. Show this to taxi drivers: "Please take me to [paste Chinese address]."

### Inform your bank

Call your bank. Tell them: "I'm traveling to China. I'll be using Alipay. Please don't block my card." This one call prevents the nightmare of your card getting frozen on day one.

### Get some RMB cash

¥500–1000. Yes, everyone says China is cashless. And for 99% of transactions, it is. But that 1% — a street food stall in a small alley, a taxi in a county-level city, a metro ticket machine that won't read your foreign card — will ruin your day if you have zero cash. Carry small bills (¥10, ¥20). Change is hard to come by.

### Buy a power bank

Your phone is your wallet, map, translator, camera, and ticket. If it dies, you're in trouble. 20,000mAh minimum. Buy it before the airport — airport electronics are overpriced.

### Pack toilet paper and wet wipes

Squat toilets are the norm outside of hotels and malls. They rarely have toilet paper. Pack a small pack of tissues and wet wipes in your day bag. This is the single most common "I wish someone had told me" from Reddit.

## 24 Hours Before: Final Checks

### Screenshot everything

eSIM QR code. Hotel booking confirmation. Visa. Passport. Flight tickets. Save them in a folder on your phone that works offline.

### Check your eSIM is active

Open your phone's cellular settings. Confirm the eSIM profile is installed and active. Turn off data roaming on your home SIM. Set the eSIM as your data line. Test it for 30 seconds before you leave for the airport.

### Download offline maps

On Apple Maps or Amap, download the offline map for Beijing, Shanghai, or wherever you're landing. If your eSIM doesn't connect immediately on arrival, you still have navigation.

### Pack your day bag strategically

Power bank on top. Toilet paper accessible. Passport in an inside pocket. Printed documents in an easy-to-reach folder. You don't want to be digging through your luggage at immigration.

## Your First Hour in China

### At immigration

You'll fill out an arrival card (on the plane or at the airport). Have your passport and printed hotel booking ready. The officer will ask where you're staying — have the address ready.

### After baggage claim

**Don't leave the airport without:**

1. Confirm your eSIM is connected and data is working
2. Open Alipay — make sure it loads and your linked card shows
3. Open your map app — does it show your location correctly?
4. Confirm your VPN connects if you need it

If any of these break, the airport has WiFi at information desks and coffee shops. Fix it before you leave.

### Getting to your hotel

**Didi (China's Uber)** works with foreign cards linked directly — no WeChat balance needed. It has built-in chat translation for communicating with your driver. This is your safest first ride.

**Metro**: QR codes for the metro live inside Alipay. Search for "乘车码" (transit code). But this sometimes requires a Chinese phone number. If it doesn't work, buy a physical ticket at the machine — that's what the ¥20 notes in your pocket are for.

**Taxi**: Show the driver your hotel address in Chinese characters. Have cash ready — not all taxis take Alipay from foreigners.

## The Bottom Line

Reddit makes China travel sound terrifying. It's not. The country is incredibly safe — solo female travelers, late-night wanderers, people who speak zero Chinese — all report the same thing: "The anxiety faded after the first 24 hours."

The difference between a smooth trip and a stressful one is almost entirely about the tech stack. If your eSIM works, your map works, and your payments work, everything else you can figure out.
